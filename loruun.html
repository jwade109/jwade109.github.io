The object of Loruun is to be as close to the ideal of language as possible.

The ideal language, it is held, has the following characteristics.

  • It is semantically and syntactically precise.
  • It is easy to print, write, read, hear, and dictate.
  • It is a potent tool for poets, novelists, scientists, and engineers alike.
  • It is accessible across cultures and age cohorts, and to machines.
  • It is expandable, adaptable, fluid.

Limitations placed on Loruun in service to these goals are as follows.

Every letter makes a noise. Words are spelled how they sound, no exceptions. There are no homonyms, no homophones, and no homographs. One word maps onto exactly one concept, full stop.

Capitalization is not used. The only non-audible symbols are the short pause, which delimits words, and a long pause, which delimits sentences. Question marks, quotation marks, exclamation points, and other frills are removed. Any important flow control structures are conveyed with spoken word. Furthermore inflection, cadence, and emphasis are semantically irrelevant.

A given word can never have more than one role in grammar. For example, the ambiguity in English between “house” the noun and “house” the verb will never be permitted in Loruun because these would be two different words.

Strict word orderings are only necessary in specific circumstances. Not all orderings of words are valid and changing the order of words can likely change the meaning of a sentence, but there are often many ways to write an equivalent sentence.

In Loruun, the animated image format is spelled “gif” and pronounced with a hard g, because no other g exists. If you want a soft g, you’d better spell it with a j.
